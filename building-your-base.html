Building Your Base

The Importance of a Running Base

Having a strong base is crucial in reducing the risk of injury during training. Overuse injuries are particularly common among runners who neglect the importance of building a foundation to their training. Studies reveal that increasing your training mileage too quickly is a major cause for concerns of common running injuries, such as shin splints, plantar fasciitis, and IT band syndrome. However, building a running base for a few months depending on current fitness gives your body enough time to adapt to the gradual increase in running intensity and load.

How to Develop a Running Base

The approach to building a running base can vary depending on the runner's experience and the distance they would like to cover. For a beginner, a suitable training plan should incorporate walk breaks and gradually build the weekly mileage. Intermediate runners may include more extended runs with strides at the end of their running miles. Advanced runners can focus on their speed and increase their weekly mileage as their base fitness improves.

Regardless of experience, every runner needs to add strength training exercises to their routine to supplement their running. Bodyweight exercises, resistance band exercises, and dumbbell exercises are recommended.

In conclusion, building a solid running base should not be overlooked regardless of experience level. With sufficient preparation for your training, you can achieve your goals and dominate your races without the risk of succumbing to injuries along the way.

Beginner Base Training Plan

Break up the run and walk intervals however feels comfortable, alternating 2 minutes running with 2 minutes walking. Start around 20 minutes total most days, as indicated below, but work up to 30 minutes total most days.

Monday: run-walk for 20 min
Tuesday: strength
Wednesday: run-walk for 20 min
Thursday: rest or strength
Friday: run-walk for 20 min
Saturday: run-walk for 30 min
Sunday: rest or strength

Intermediate Base Training Plan

Each run should be an easy, conversational effort. But on two days, add four to six strides, with 40-60 seconds rest between, at the end of your miles. Run your strides at 80 to 90 percent maximum effort at the tail end of your run with a focus on smooth and relaxed running form.

Monday: run for 30 min + strength
Tuesday: run for 40 min
Wednesday: run for 50 min
Thursday: strength
Friday: run for 40 min
Saturday: run for 60 min
Sunday: rest
